Key principles Joe has established for supplier negotiation emails:

**1. Always anchor. The power of anchoring cannot be understated.**
- State a specific, non-round number (e.g. $1.79, not $1.80) -- signals research
- Restate the anchor in every exchange until the supplier engages with it
- If you soften the tone, never drop the anchor -- tone and number are independent
- The ask and the accept are two different numbers. Write the ask; mentally hold the accept.

**2. Face-saving is critical. Don't "pants" the supplier.**
- Chinese business culture (and most cultures): humiliating a contact in writing damages the relationship across ALL product lines, not just the one you're negotiating
- If your supplier contact (like Lu) has to take your email to their boss (like GM Michael), the boss will see everything. What the boss reads FIRST sets the tone.
- Analytical leverage (raw material cost math, margin analysis) is most powerful when HELD, not shown. The fact that you know is the weapon. Showing your work in writing is deploying the weapon.
- If you must show the math, frame it as YOUR internal analysis, not a critique of THEIR quote. "Our model suggests X" is collaborative. "Your numbers don't reconcile" is confrontational.
- Give the supplier an exit ramp: "There may be something we are missing" or "We may have something wrong in our assumptions." They can then "recalculate" or "find an error" without admitting they padded.

**3. Be ruthless on the number, gracious on the form.**
- You can open with gratitude (thanking them for concessions on other workstreams) and still hit them with a hard price ask
- Don't soften the number to soften the tone. Those are two separate levers.
- Don't confuse being polite with being weak. The most dangerous negotiator is the one who makes you feel respected while asking for everything.

**4. Don't make the supplier lift too much.**
- Asking for three volume tier quotes = too much work, triggers defensiveness
- One alternate volume is enough. Let them produce one number.
- Keep the email short and focused. Every extra paragraph is another thing to argue about.

**5. Save analytical leverage for round 2.**
- If the first reply is soft (warm opener, bottom-line price ask, no math), and the supplier's counter is still weak, THEN deploy the raw material analysis in round 2
- This is more effective because: (a) they've already shown willingness to negotiate, (b) the math becomes a closing tool not an opening shot, (c) the relationship survived round 1

**6. Volume-for-price trades are powerful.**
- "We'll come up from $X to $Y in exchange for Z-fold increase in quantity"
- This is a clear, structured trade that both sides can evaluate
- It gives the supplier a reason to accept the lower price (more revenue)
- Phrase as "first order" to imply future volume without committing

**Why:** Joe corrected my initial drafts multiple times. I was too aggressive on raw material call-outs, asked for too many volume tiers, and lost the anchor when I softened the tone. These corrections reflect Joe's actual negotiation philosophy: analytical rigor behind the scenes, grace on the surface, iron on the price.

**How to apply:** Every supplier email draft should be checked against all six principles. Especially watch for: dropping the anchor when softening tone, showing too much analytical work in writing, making the supplier do too much work in their response, and forgetting face-saving dynamics.
